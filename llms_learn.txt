## Learn — Knowledge Base (open, CC BY 4.0)
Knowledge base on compact low-neutron fusion. Design point + open 81-sim record (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21248916).

### Concept encyclopedia
- [Negative Triangularity](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/negative-triangularity.html): Negative triangularity (δ = −0.30) shapes the plasma cross-section to suppress edge instabilities — the design invariant at the core of the Kronos MetroVolt architecture.
- [Bootstrap Fraction](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/bootstrap-fraction.html): The bootstrap fraction (f_BS) is the share of plasma current the plasma drives itself — reducing the external current-drive power a fusion plant must pay for.
- [The Greenwald Density Limit](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/greenwald-limit.html): The Greenwald limit is an empirical ceiling on tokamak plasma density. Negative-triangularity operation lets Kronos run above the conventional fraction.
- [REBCO High-Temperature Superconducting Magnets](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/rebco-hts-magnets.html): REBCO high-temperature superconductor lets a compact tokamak reach very high magnetic field. Kronos MetroVolt runs a 24.6 T peak-field graded winding pack.
- [Direct Energy Conversion (DEC)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/direct-energy-conversion.html): Direct energy conversion turns charged fusion products straight into electricity, bypassing the steam cycle. It is the reason Kronos pairs DEC with a low-neutron fuel.
- [The Lawson Criterion & Triple Product](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/lawson-criterion.html): The Lawson criterion sets the density-temperature-confinement product a plasma must reach to sustain net fusion. Here is how it frames the Kronos design.
- [Safety Factor (q₉₅)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/safety-factor-q95.html): The safety factor q₉₅ measures how tightly magnetic field lines wind around a tokamak — a primary stability margin. Kronos holds an equilibrium-computed q₉₅ ≥ 5.0 floor.
- [Hot-Ion Mode](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/hot-ion-mode.html): In a hot-ion plasma the ions are hotter than the electrons (T_i/T_e > 1), boosting fusion output. Kronos treats this posture as a bet it must prove, not assume.
- [Tokamak](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/tokamak.html): A tokamak confines fusion plasma in a magnetic torus. Kronos MetroVolt is a high-field, negative-triangularity spherical tokamak.
- [Deuterium](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/deuterium.html): Deuterium is a heavy, stable isotope of hydrogen abundant in seawater — one half of the Kronos staged D–³He fuel cycle.
- [Helium-3](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/helium-3.html): Helium-3 is a rare, light helium isotope and the second half of Kronos's D–³He fuel — bred in situ rather than mined.
- [Tritium](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/tritium.html): Tritium is a radioactive hydrogen isotope central to conventional D–T fusion. Kronos minimizes it, using it only for commissioning and as a ³He precursor.
- [Bremsstrahlung Radiation](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/bremsstrahlung.html): Bremsstrahlung is 'braking radiation' emitted as electrons scatter off ions — a dominant energy-loss channel Kronos budgets at 498 MW.
- [Synchrotron Radiation](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/synchrotron-radiation.html): Synchrotron radiation is emitted as electrons spiral in the magnetic field. At high field it matters — Kronos budgets 51 MW and relies on wall reflectivity.
- [Plasma Beta (β)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-beta.html): Beta measures how much plasma pressure a magnetic field holds — an efficiency and stability metric. Kronos runs β_N = 4.33 with a ≤ 4.5 clamp.
- [Elongation (κ)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/elongation.html): Elongation stretches the plasma cross-section vertically for higher current and pressure. Kronos runs a strongly shaped κ = 2.56.
- [The Divertor](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-divertor.html): The divertor is the plasma's exhaust system, handling immense heat flux. Kronos relies on detachment and Super-X flux expansion — a stated open item.
- [Edge-Localized Modes (ELMs)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/edge-localized-modes.html): ELMs are violent edge instabilities that can destroy plasma-facing hardware. Kronos avoids them entirely with an ELM-free negative-triangularity edge.
- [H-mode and L-mode](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/h-mode-and-l-mode.html): H-mode is a high-confinement regime with a dangerous edge; L-mode is quieter but leakier. Kronos uses negative triangularity to get the best of both.
- [Aspect Ratio](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/aspect-ratio.html): Aspect ratio is the ratio of a tokamak's major to minor radius. Kronos runs a low A = 2.0 — the spherical regime — for stability and pressure efficiency.
- [Energy Confinement Time (τ_E)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/energy-confinement-time.html): τ_E measures how long a plasma holds its heat. Confinement scalings and the required H98 = 1.84 multiplier are expressed against it.
- [Magnetic Confinement](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/magnetic-confinement.html): Magnetic confinement uses powerful fields to hold a fusion plasma away from any wall. It is the approach behind tokamaks like Kronos MetroVolt.
- [Stellarator](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/stellarator.html): A stellarator confines plasma with twisted external coils and no plasma current — disruption-free but complex. Kronos chose the tokamak path.
- [Plasma Disruption](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-disruption.html): A disruption is the sudden, violent loss of plasma confinement — the defining hazard of a tokamak. Kronos budgets a 10 GJ event at 43 MA.
- [Resistive Wall Mode (RWM)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/resistive-wall-mode.html): The RWM is a slow-growing instability at high beta that the conducting wall only partly suppresses. Kronos holds it with active feedback.
- [The Troyon Beta Limit](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/troyon-limit.html): The Troyon limit caps normalized beta before instability. It is the binding constraint on the Kronos design point at β_N = 4.33.
- [Scrape-Off Layer (SOL)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/scrape-off-layer.html): The SOL is the thin plasma layer beyond the last closed flux surface that carries exhaust to the divertor. Kronos widens it with Super-X geometry.
- [Plasma-Facing Components (PFCs)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-facing-components.html): PFCs are the materials that directly face the plasma — first wall and divertor. They must survive extreme heat, particles, and radiation.
- [Hafnium Carbide First Wall](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/hafnium-carbide-wall.html): Kronos uses a laser-fused hafnium carbide first wall — a refractory, highly reflective surface that recycles synchrotron radiation.
- [Fast-Ion Channeling (P6)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fast-ion-channeling.html): Fast-ion channeling redirects energetic-ion energy into the plasma fuel ions — a key Kronos upside, bounded and gated as prediction P6.
- [Neutron Activation](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/neutron-activation.html): Neutron activation is how fusion neutrons make structures mildly radioactive. A low-neutron design minimizes it — Kronos's central materials advantage.
- [Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/levelized-cost-of-electricity.html): LCOE is the lifetime cost per unit of electricity generated. Kronos publishes a full LCOE ladder rather than a single headline number.
- [Wright's Law](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/wrights-law.html): Wright's law says unit cost falls a fixed percentage each time cumulative production doubles — the engine behind Kronos's LCOE decline.
- [Current Drive & Non-Inductive Operation](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/current-drive.html): Current drive sustains the plasma current without a transformer, enabling steady-state operation. Kronos runs a high non-inductive fraction.
- [Plasma Current (I_p)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-current.html): The plasma current generates the confining poloidal field and sets many limits. Kronos runs a high I_p = 42.5 MA — its least-forgiving parameter.
- [Plasma Heating](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-heating.html): Fusion plasmas must reach 100+ million degrees. External heating — neutral beams and radio-frequency waves — brings them there and drives current.
- [Fusion Gain (Q)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-gain-q.html): Q is the ratio of fusion power produced to heating power supplied. Kronos is honest that its endpoint is a driven system, not an igniting one.
- [Engineering Gain (Q_eng)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/engineering-gain.html): Q_eng compares net electricity out to all electrical power in — the number that actually matters for a power plant. Kronos's ceiling is Q_eng ≈ 2.7.
- [Ignition vs Driven Operation](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/ignition-vs-driven.html): An igniting plasma sustains itself; a driven one needs continuous heating. Kronos designs to a driven operating point and says so plainly.
- [Proton–Boron-11 (p–¹¹B) Fusion](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/proton-boron-11.html): p–¹¹B is the truly aneutronic fusion reaction — the gated research endpoint of the Kronos fuel roadmap, not its commercial baseline.
- [Catalyzed D–D Fuel Cycle](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/catalyzed-d-d.html): In a catalyzed D–D cycle, deuterium fuses with itself and the products (³He and tritium) are burned in turn — how Kronos breeds its own helium-3.
- [Fusion Power Density](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-power-density.html): Fusion power density rises steeply with magnetic field and pressure — the physics that lets a high-field compact tokamak rival a much larger one.
- [Flux Surfaces & the Separatrix](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/flux-surfaces.html): Nested magnetic flux surfaces confine the plasma; the separatrix is the last closed one. Together they define where the plasma ends and exhaust begins.
- [Fusion Neutronics](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-neutronics.html): Neutronics is the study of how fusion neutrons travel, deposit energy, and activate materials. For a low-neutron plant, the numbers are far gentler.
- [Why Kronos Needs No Breeding Blanket](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/no-breeding-blanket.html): D–T reactors need a lithium breeding blanket that wears out and limits availability. Kronos's D–³He fuel removes that whole subsystem.
- [High-Temperature Superconductivity](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/high-temperature-superconductivity.html): HTS materials carry lossless current at high field and warmer temperatures than old superconductors — the breakthrough enabling compact high-field fusion.
- [Magnet Quench & Protection](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/magnet-quench.html): A quench is when part of a superconducting magnet suddenly goes resistive, dumping huge stored energy. Protecting against it is critical magnet engineering.
- [Cryogenics & Magnet Cooling](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/cryogenics.html): Superconducting magnets must be kept cold. HTS lets Kronos run near 20 K rather than 4 K — a meaningful engineering and efficiency easing.
- [Capacity Factor](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/capacity-factor.html): Capacity factor is the fraction of the time a plant actually delivers its rated power. Firm fusion targets a high one; D–T is dragged down by blanket downtime.
- [Grid Firming & Dispatchable Power](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/grid-firming.html): A firm, dispatchable generator delivers power on demand regardless of weather — the role Kronos designs MetroVolt to fill alongside renewables.
- [Gyrokinetic Turbulence](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/gyrokinetic-turbulence.html): Gyrokinetics models the small-scale turbulence that drains heat from a plasma. The nonlinear gyrokinetic check on Kronos confinement is the outstanding verification.
- [Ion-Temperature-Gradient (ITG) Turbulence](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/itg-turbulence.html): ITG modes are the dominant heat-loss turbulence in hot plasmas. Running the ions hotter than the electrons raises the ITG threshold — a key Kronos lever.
- [Alfvén Eigenmodes (TAEs)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/alfven-eigenmodes.html): Fast fusion ions can excite Alfvén-wave instabilities that expel them before they heat the plasma. Kronos flags TAE transport as a named open channel.
- [The Kink Instability](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/kink-instability.html): The kink is a current-driven instability that can disrupt a tokamak. A safety factor above ~2 (Kronos holds q95 ≥ 5) keeps it at bay.
- [Ballooning & Mercier Stability](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/ballooning-mercier.html): Ballooning and Mercier modes are pressure-driven instabilities that cap how much pressure a plasma can hold. Kronos checks its second-stability access.
- [Vertical Displacement Event (VDE)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/vertical-displacement-event.html): A tall, elongated plasma is vertically unstable and can drift into the wall — a VDE. Kronos holds κ = 2.56 with a control margin of 2.02.
- [Runaway Electrons](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/runaway-electrons.html): During a disruption, electrons can be accelerated to near light speed, forming a damaging beam. Runaway mitigation at 43 MA is a Kronos open item.
- [The Plasma Pedestal](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-plasma-pedestal.html): The pedestal is the steep edge pressure barrier of H-mode — the thing that drives ELMs. Kronos's L-mode-like NT edge deliberately does without a strong one.
- [Neoclassical Transport](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/neoclassical-transport.html): Neoclassical transport is the irreducible collisional leakage from a torus's geometry — and the source of the useful bootstrap current.
- [Gyroradius & ρ*](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/gyroradius.html): Charged particles spiral with a gyroradius set by field and temperature. Its normalized value ρ* governs how experiments scale to a reactor.
- [Fusion Cross-Section & Reactivity](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-cross-section.html): The cross-section measures how likely two nuclei are to fuse at a given energy. D–³He peaks at higher temperature than D–T — shaping the Kronos operating point.
- [Toroidal Field Coils](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/toroidal-field-coils.html): The toroidal field coils produce the main confining field. In a spherical tokamak their inboard legs crowd the center stack at peak field.
- [The Center Stack](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-center-stack.html): The center stack is the crowded central column of a spherical tokamak, holding the inboard magnet and shield in minimal space — a low-neutron design's advantage.
- [Demountable Magnets](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/demountable-magnets.html): Demountable joints let a fusion magnet be opened for maintenance and assembly — a practical advantage of REBCO that Kronos builds into its winding pack.
- [The Neutron Shield](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-neutron-shield.html): The shield stops fusion neutrons from damaging the magnet and activating the structure. A low-neutron plant needs far less of it — a compounding advantage.
- [Charged-Particle Direct Converter (CPDC)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/charged-particle-direct-converter.html): A CPDC decelerates charged fusion products against an electric field, recovering their energy directly as electricity — a core mode of the Kronos DEC train.
- [Plasma-Exhaust MHD Generator](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/mhd-generator.html): An MHD generator extracts electricity from flowing conducting plasma exhaust with no moving parts — one mode of the Kronos multi-modal converter.
- [Thermionic Energy Conversion](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/thermionic-conversion.html): Thermionic converters turn heat directly into electricity by boiling electrons off a hot surface — a solid-state stage in the Kronos DEC train.
- [Photonic Energy Capture](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/photonic-energy-capture.html): Photonic capture recovers energy from the radiation a plasma emits — bremsstrahlung and synchrotron light — as another stage of the Kronos converter.
- [The Carnot Limit](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/carnot-limit.html): The Carnot limit caps the efficiency of any heat engine. Direct energy conversion sidesteps it entirely — the efficiency case for the Kronos approach.
- [The Main Fusion Reactions](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-main-fusion-reactions.html): D–T, D–D, D–³He, and p–¹¹B are the fusion reactions that matter for power. Kronos's staged path runs down this list toward lower neutron output.
- [Helium Ash & Exhaust](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/helium-ash.html): Fusion produces helium 'ash' that must be pumped out before it dilutes the fuel. The divertor and pumping system handle it in the Kronos design.
- [The Staged Fuel Cycle](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fuel-staging.html): Kronos runs fusion fuels in stages — from D–T commissioning to a ³He-rich optimum toward the p–¹¹B endpoint — trading difficulty for ever-lower neutrons.
- [Direct Internal Recovery (DIR)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/direct-internal-recovery.html): DIR recovers and recycles unburned fuel within the plant on a single pass. Kronos targets >95% single-pass extraction — pre-registered prediction P4.
- [The Vacuum Vessel](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/vacuum-vessel.html): The vacuum vessel holds the ultra-high vacuum the plasma needs and forms the first safety boundary — sitting between plasma and magnets.
- [Balance of Plant](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/balance-of-plant.html): Balance of plant is everything outside the reactor core — cooling, power conditioning, controls. Direct energy conversion shrinks it versus a steam plant.
- [The Super-X Divertor](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/super-x-divertor.html): A Super-X divertor lengthens and spreads the exhaust leg to ease heat load, enabling the detachment Kronos relies on. Its flux expansion is within demonstrated range.
- [Plasma Diagnostics](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-diagnostics.html): Diagnostics are the sensors that measure a plasma in real time. Kronos's diagnostic constellation feeds an AI control stack that holds the plasma's shape.
- [The Central Solenoid](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/central-solenoid.html): The central solenoid is the transformer coil that ramps the plasma current at startup. Its flux demand sets part of the machine's startup budget.
- [Poloidal Field & Shaping Coils](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/poloidal-field-coils.html): Poloidal field coils position and shape the plasma — setting its elongation, triangularity, and vertical stability. They hold the Kronos NT shape.
- [The S.M.A.R.T. Architecture](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/smart-architecture.html): S.M.A.R.T. — Superconducting Min-Aspect-Ratio Torus — is the shared Kronos platform behind both MetroVolt and AEGIS. It is the company's core patented design.
- [The Gate Program (G1–G3)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-gate-program.html): Kronos advances through pre-registered gates, each retiring a specific risk. G1 tests confinement, G2 the magnet, G3 the fuel loop.
- [The Falsifiable Seven](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-falsifiable-seven.html): Seven pre-registered predictions (P1–P7) that Kronos commits to test at the gates and publish regardless of outcome — the core of its 'shows its work' discipline.
- [MetroVolt — The Commercial Generator](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/metrovolt.html): MetroVolt is Kronos's commercial fusion generator — city-scale, firm, low-neutron power built on the S.M.A.R.T. architecture.
- [AEGIS — The Defense Generator](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/aegis.html): AEGIS is Kronos's defense-oriented fusion generator — resilient, islandable power for defense applications, on the same S.M.A.R.T. architecture as MetroVolt.
- [The Digital Twin](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-digital-twin.html): A digital twin is a live, physics-based model running alongside the plant, predicting its state 50–100 ms ahead so the controller can act before events arrive.
- [Open Data & Reproducibility](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/open-data-reproducibility.html): Kronos releases its full simulation record — 81 analyses, code and data — openly under CC BY 4.0, so anyone can check the numbers behind the design.
- [The Graded Winding Pack](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/graded-winding-pack.html): Kronos grades its magnet winding pack layer by layer to the local field, keeping strain within margin at 24.6 T while staying demountable.
- [Wall Loading & Heat Flux](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/wall-loading.html): Wall loading is the power per unit area the first wall must handle. Kronos's low-neutron design keeps neutron wall loading unusually gentle.
- [Displacements Per Atom (dpa)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/displacements-per-atom.html): dpa measures neutron damage to materials — how many times each atom is knocked from its lattice site. Kronos's low-neutron wall stays within its qualification.
- [Tritium Inventory & Safety](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/tritium-inventory.html): Tritium is radioactive and regulated, so the on-site amount matters. Kronos's D–³He design keeps its tritium inventory small compared with a D–T plant.
- [Impurity Seeding & the Radiative Divertor](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/impurity-seeding.html): Deliberately adding trace impurities makes the exhaust radiate its heat away before it reaches the target — how Kronos achieves divertor detachment.
- [Lithium-6 & the No-Breeding-Claim](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/lithium-6-no-breeding-claim.html): Some concepts invoke lithium-6 breeding. Kronos deliberately makes no such claim — its fuel is staged D–³He, and it says so plainly.
- [Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/power-purchase-agreement.html): A PPA is a long-term contract to buy a plant's electricity at a set price. Kronos's required PPA band is $56–92/MWh.
- [The Sawtooth Instability](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/sawtooth-instability.html): Sawteeth are periodic, benign relaxations of the plasma core that flatten the central profiles. They are a managed feature, not a threat, in tokamak operation.
- [Steady-State Operation](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/steady-state-operation.html): A power plant must run continuously, not in pulses. Kronos reaches steady state with a high bootstrap fraction plus non-inductive current drive.
- [Plasma Startup & the Ramp Phase](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-startup.html): Starting a tokamak means breaking down the gas, ramping the current, and heating to burn — a choreographed sequence Kronos budgets flux and power for.
- [Confinement Scaling Laws (IPB98)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/confinement-scaling-laws.html): Because confinement can't be computed exactly, the field uses empirical scaling laws. Kronos quotes its requirement as H98 over the IPB98(y,2) standard.
- [The Fusion Triple Product](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-fusion-triple-product.html): The triple product of density, temperature, and confinement time is the single figure of merit for how close a plasma is to net fusion.
- [Plasma Rotation & Flow Shear](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-rotation.html): A rotating plasma with sheared flow suppresses turbulence and helps stabilize instabilities — a lever that supports the Kronos confinement and stability case.
- [Neoclassical Tearing Modes (NTMs)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/neoclassical-tearing-modes.html): NTMs are magnetic islands seeded by the bootstrap current that degrade confinement. They are controllable with localized current drive.
- [Magnetic Islands](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/magnetic-islands.html): Magnetic islands are chains of closed loops that break the smooth nesting of flux surfaces, short-circuiting confinement across them.
- [Electron Bernstein Wave (EBW) Heating](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/electron-bernstein-wave.html): EBW heating reaches the dense core of a spherical tokamak where ordinary microwaves can't penetrate — a natural heating and current-drive tool for Kronos.
- [High-Harmonic Fast Wave (HHFW)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/high-harmonic-fast-wave.html): HHFW is a radio-frequency method for heating and driving current in spherical tokamaks — and the launcher Kronos proposes for the fast-ion channeling test.
- [Neutral Beam Injection (NBI)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/neutral-beam-injection.html): NBI fires fast neutral atoms into the plasma to heat it, drive current, and set rotation — a workhorse tool with multiple roles in the Kronos design.
- [Inertial Confinement Fusion](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/inertial-confinement-fusion.html): Inertial confinement compresses a fuel pellet with lasers for a brief burst — the main alternative to the magnetic approach Kronos uses.
- [Energy Return on Investment (EROI)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/energy-return-on-investment.html): EROI is the ratio of energy a plant delivers over its life to the energy invested to build and run it — a measure fusion aims to score highly on.
- [Fuel Abundance & Energy Security](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fuel-abundance-energy-security.html): Kronos's fuels are effectively inexhaustible and domestically sourced — deuterium from seawater, helium-3 bred in situ — with no import dependence.
- [Plant Lifetime (30 Full-Power-Years)](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plant-lifetime.html): Kronos designs and prices MetroVolt on a 30 full-power-year basis — long enough that the low-neutron first wall lasts without replacement.
- [Remote Maintenance](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/remote-maintenance.html): Activated reactor components must be serviced remotely. A low-neutron design with demountable magnets makes maintenance faster and cheaper for Kronos.
- [Fusion & Decarbonization](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-and-decarbonization.html): Firm, zero-carbon fusion fills the gap intermittent renewables leave — the always-on clean power a fully decarbonized grid needs.
- [Pellet Fuelling](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/pellet-fuelling.html): Firing frozen fuel pellets deep into the plasma core keeps the density and reaction rate up — the main core-fuelling method for a reactor.
- [Effective Charge (Z_eff) & Plasma Purity](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/effective-charge-zeff.html): Z_eff measures how much impurity contaminates a plasma. Keeping it low protects fusion rate and limits radiation — a constant operational goal.
- [Error Fields & Correction](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/error-fields.html): Tiny imperfections in the magnets create error fields that can lock modes and trigger disruptions. Correction coils cancel them.
- [Water Use & Siting](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/water-use-and-siting.html): A direct-conversion, thermally-light fusion plant uses far less cooling water than a steam station — widening where it can be sited.
- [Grid Inertia & Stability Services](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/grid-inertia.html): Grids need inertia and stability services that intermittent renewables don't provide. A firm fusion generator can supply them.
- [Fusion Waste & Decommissioning](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-waste.html): Fusion produces no long-lived spent fuel — only activated structures that decay to recyclable levels. A low-neutron plant produces even less.
- [Ion & Electron Temperature](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/ion-and-electron-temperature.html): Fusion depends on the ion temperature; the electrons are where losses concentrate. Kronos deliberately runs the ions hotter than the electrons.
- [Plasma Density & Fuelling](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-density-and-fuelling.html): Fusion power rises with the square of density, so running dense is valuable. Kronos operates above the Greenwald limit, sustained by careful fuelling.
- [Biological Shielding & Site Safety](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/biological-shielding.html): Biological shielding protects workers and the public from radiation during operation. A low-neutron plant needs less of it and poses no evacuation-scale risk.

### Questions people ask
- [Does D–³He fusion produce neutrons?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/does-d-he3-produce-neutrons.html): The primary D–³He reaction is aneutronic, but side D–D reactions make some neutrons — which is why Kronos says 'low-neutron,' not 'aneutronic.'
- [Where does helium-3 come from?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/where-does-helium-3-come-from.html): Helium-3 is scarce on Earth. Kronos runs a staged, catalyzed D–³He cycle that breeds much of its own ³He, so the plant is not hostage to thin external supply.
- [Can fusion provide firm, baseload power?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-fusion-provide-baseload-power.html): Firm, dispatchable power is the whole point of the Kronos design — city-scale electricity that runs day and night, independent of weather.
- [What will fusion electricity cost?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-does-fusion-electricity-cost.html): Kronos publishes a full LCOE ladder — FOAK $84–92/MWh falling to $48–56/MWh at fleet scale — and explicitly declines to claim the sub-$40 numbers others quote.
- [Is fusion power safe?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-fusion-power-safe.html): Fusion cannot melt down or run away, and a low-neutron design minimizes activation and long-lived waste. Here is the honest safety picture for the Kronos design.
- [What is a spherical tokamak?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-a-spherical-tokamak.html): A spherical tokamak squeezes the donut into a cored apple — a low aspect ratio that improves stability and efficiency. Kronos MetroVolt runs at aspect ratio 2.0.
- [Why does Kronos use negative triangularity?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-negative-triangularity.html): Three load-bearing reasons: an ELM-free edge that protects the converter, H-mode-grade core confinement with an L-mode edge, and density headroom.
- [What is nuclear fusion?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-nuclear-fusion.html): Nuclear fusion is the joining of light atomic nuclei to release energy — the process that powers the sun, and the basis of the Kronos generators.
- [How is fusion different from nuclear fission?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-fusion-differ-from-fission.html): Fusion joins light nuclei; fission splits heavy ones. Fusion has no chain reaction, cannot melt down, and leaves no long-lived waste.
- [Why is fusion energy so hard to achieve?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-is-fusion-so-hard.html): Fusion requires heating fuel to over 100 million degrees and confining it well enough to produce net energy — the Lawson condition. Every fusion design is a way to meet it.
- [When will fusion actually power the grid?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/when-will-fusion-power-the-grid.html): Kronos advances on a gated schedule rather than a marketing date — the G1 confinement testbed is roughly four years to first plasma, with each gate retiring a specific risk.
- [Is fusion energy clean and renewable?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-fusion-energy-clean.html): Fusion emits no CO₂, produces no long-lived waste, and runs on effectively inexhaustible fuel — clean by any measure, and effectively renewable.
- [Can a fusion reactor explode or melt down?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-a-fusion-reactor-melt-down.html): No. Fusion has no chain reaction and holds only seconds of fuel — any interruption stops the reaction. There is no meltdown or explosion pathway.
- [How hot is a fusion plasma?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-hot-is-a-fusion-plasma.html): A fusion plasma runs at over 100 million degrees — hotter than the sun's core. Kronos targets central ion temperatures around 65–70 keV, roughly 750 million °C.
- [What fuels does fusion use?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-fuels-does-fusion-use.html): Most fusion uses deuterium–tritium. Kronos uses low-neutron deuterium–helium-3, with deuterium from seawater and helium-3 bred in the plant itself.
- [How does a fusion plant actually make electricity?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-fusion-make-electricity.html): Most fusion concepts boil water for a steam turbine. Kronos captures charged-particle energy directly as electricity, skipping the steam cycle entirely.
- [What is Kronos Fusion Energy?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-kronos-fusion-energy.html): Kronos Fusion Energy is a company building compact, low-neutron fusion generators — MetroVolt for commercial power and AEGIS for defense — engineered in the open.
- [What makes Kronos different from other fusion companies?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-makes-kronos-different.html): Low-neutron D–³He fuel, direct energy conversion, negative triangularity, and radical openness — Kronos publishes its data, predictions, and open questions.

- [Why does Kronos use helium-3 instead of tritium?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-does-kronos-use-helium-3.html): Helium-3 gives a low-neutron reaction whose charged products can be converted directly to electricity — and Kronos breeds its own, sidestepping the scarcity problem.
- [Is Kronos fusion aneutronic?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-kronos-fusion-aneutronic.html): No — Kronos is 'low-neutron,' not aneutronic. The D–³He primary reaction is aneutronic, but side D–D reactions make some neutrons. Only the gated p–¹¹B endpoint is truly aneutronic.

- [Why does Kronos use such a strong magnetic field?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-such-a-strong-magnetic-field.html): Fusion power density rises with the fourth power of magnetic field, so a 24.6 T machine can be far more compact than a low-field one of equal output.
- [How does Kronos handle plasma disruptions?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-kronos-handle-disruptions.html): Kronos reduces disruption likelihood with an ELM-free edge and strong control, and budgets the loads of a 10 GJ event — while stating runaway mitigation as an open item.
- [What is direct energy conversion?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-direct-energy-conversion.html): Direct energy conversion captures the energy of charged fusion products straight as electricity, skipping the steam turbine — a defining feature of the Kronos design.

Kronos extends demonstrated results — DIII-D negative triangularity, 24.4 T HTS coils — and treats the remaining work as gated engineering and validation.
- [How much does a fusion plant cost?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-much-does-a-fusion-plant-cost.html): Kronos publishes a levelized-cost ladder — FOAK $84–92/MWh falling to $48–56/MWh at fleet scale — rather than a single capital number, and declines the sub-$40 figures others quote.
- [Is fusion competitive with solar and wind?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-fusion-competitive-with-renewables.html): Fusion competes on firmness, not raw cost. Its value is dispatchable, always-on power — the thing intermittent renewables can't provide and increasingly must pay for.
- [Can fusion power a data center?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-fusion-power-a-data-center.html): Yes — a firm, DC-native fusion generator is unusually well matched to the large, constant, 24/7 load of an AI data center. Kronos designs MetroVolt for exactly this.
- [How much electricity does one MetroVolt produce?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-much-electricity-does-one-metrovolt-produce.html): A MetroVolt unit is designed for 0.61–0.84 GWe of net electric output; a three-unit station reaches roughly 1.8–2.5 GWe — city-scale firm power.
- [Does fusion produce radioactive waste?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/does-fusion-produce-radioactive-waste.html): Fusion produces no long-lived spent fuel — only activated structures that decay to recyclable levels in about a century. A low-neutron plant produces even less.
- [Is tritium dangerous?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-tritium-dangerous.html): Tritium is radioactive but emits only weak radiation and is managed routinely in industry. Kronos's D–³He design keeps its tritium inventory small.
- [Can fusion energy be used to make weapons?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-fusion-be-used-for-weapons.html): A fusion power plant produces no weapons-usable material — no plutonium, no enriched uranium. Low-neutron D–³He minimizes even the indirect proliferation concern.
- [What is AEGIS used for?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-aegis-used-for.html): AEGIS is Kronos's defense-oriented fusion generator — resilient, islandable power for mission-critical and defense applications, on the same architecture as MetroVolt.
- [Why is fusion valuable for defense?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-is-fusion-valuable-for-defense.html): Fusion offers energy security without fuel convoys — firm, islandable, domestically-fueled power that removes a major logistical vulnerability.
- [How can I partner with Kronos?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-can-i-partner-with-kronos.html): Kronos works through strategic partnerships — offtake, siting, technology, and defense collaboration. The contact page is the front door.
- [How does a tokamak work?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-a-tokamak-work.html): A tokamak confines fusion plasma in a magnetic bottle shaped like a doughnut, using a combination of external coils and a current in the plasma itself.
- [What is plasma?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-plasma.html): Plasma is the fourth state of matter — a hot, ionized gas of free electrons and nuclei. It's what fusion fuel becomes at millions of degrees, and what stars are made of.
- [How do you confine something that hot?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-do-you-confine-a-plasma.html): You don't touch it — you hold it in a magnetic field. A fusion plasma is confined by magnetic forces, never in contact with any material wall.
- [How does the reactor survive the heat?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-the-reactor-survive-the-heat.html): The plasma core never touches the wall. The wall handles a manageable edge flux, exhaust goes to the divertor, and low-neutron fuel limits material damage.
- [Does fusion use lasers?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/does-fusion-use-lasers.html): Some fusion does — inertial confinement uses lasers to compress fuel. Kronos uses magnetic confinement instead, holding the plasma in a magnetic field.
- [Can fusion really work on Earth?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-fusion-really-work-on-earth.html): Yes — fusion has been achieved many times in labs. The challenge isn't whether it works, but making it net-positive, continuous, and economical.
- [Isn't fusion always 30 years away?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/isnt-fusion-always-30-years-away.html): That reputation came from decades of underfunding and overpromising. Kronos avoids date-promises entirely, advancing through pre-registered gates with published results.
- [Why should I believe Kronos's numbers?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-should-i-believe-kronos-numbers.html): Because you don't have to take them on faith — Kronos publishes the full simulation code and data, pre-registers falsifiable predictions, and names its own open questions.
- [What would prove the Kronos design wrong?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-would-prove-kronos-wrong.html): Specific, pre-registered results would. If the G1 testbed shows negative-triangularity confinement below the required threshold, the design must be re-sized — and Kronos would publish it.
- [What is the G1 confinement testbed?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-g1-testbed.html): G1 is a sub-scale spherical tokamak — the first Kronos gate — built to measure the design's load-bearing confinement and hot-ion predictions before scaling up.
- [Why does Kronos publish all its data?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-does-kronos-publish-everything.html): Openness is Kronos's credibility and partnership strategy — publishing code, data, and predictions lets anyone check the work, and invites collaboration on the merits.
- [How does Kronos compare to ITER?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-kronos-compare-to-iter.html): ITER is a large public D–T science tokamak; Kronos is a compact, high-field, low-neutron commercial design. They're different bets — and Kronos builds on the physics ITER-class research produced.
- [Does fusion produce any CO₂?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/does-fusion-produce-co2.html): A fusion plant emits no CO₂ while generating power. The only carbon is the one-time embodied emissions of construction, as with any power plant.
- [How much land does a fusion plant need?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-much-land-does-a-fusion-plant-need.html): A fusion plant is compact for its output — high power density means a small footprint compared with the land solar or wind needs for equivalent firm energy.
- [What happens to the helium a fusion plant makes?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-happens-to-the-helium.html): Fusion produces helium as its 'ash' — an inert, harmless gas that is pumped out. Its energy is captured first by the direct-energy converter.
- [What is a fusion reactor made of?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-a-fusion-reactor-made-of.html): A fusion reactor is built around superconducting magnets, a refractory first wall, a vacuum vessel and shield, and — for Kronos — a direct-energy-conversion train.
- [Why do neutrons matter in fusion?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-do-neutrons-matter-in-fusion.html): Neutrons carry energy but no charge — they damage materials, activate structures, and can't be converted directly. Minimizing them is the core Kronos advantage.
- [What does 'low-neutron' mean?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-does-low-neutron-mean.html): Low-neutron means most of the fusion energy emerges as charged particles, not neutrons. Kronos's neutron fraction is ~5.25% — about 25× below deuterium–tritium.
- [What is a superconductor, and why does fusion need one?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-a-superconductor.html): A superconductor carries electricity with zero resistance. High-temperature superconductors make the strong, efficient magnets that compact fusion depends on.
- [How strong is a 24-tesla magnet?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-strong-is-a-24-tesla-magnet.html): Very strong — roughly ten times a hospital MRI and about half a million times Earth's magnetic field. That field strength is what lets a compact tokamak work.
- [Can fusion scale fast enough to matter for climate?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-fusion-scale-fast-enough-for-climate.html): Only if it's manufacturable. Kronos designs for compact, repeatable units whose cost falls with volume — the model that let solar and batteries scale.
- [How many fusion plants would it take to matter?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-many-fusion-plants-would-we-need.html): Global electricity demand is measured in terawatts, so meaningful fusion deployment means thousands of gigawatt-scale units — which is why manufacturability is everything.
- [Can a fusion plant follow demand?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-a-fusion-plant-follow-demand.html): A fusion plant is firm and dispatchable — ideal for steady base load, and able to adjust output to help balance the grid.
- [What happens if a fusion plant loses power?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-happens-if-a-fusion-plant-loses-power.html): It shuts down safely on its own. A fusion plasma needs continuous input to exist, so losing power stops the reaction — the opposite of a fission decay-heat risk.
- [Is it safe to build fusion near cities?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-it-safe-to-build-fusion-near-cities.html): Yes. Fusion has no runaway-accident pathway and no evacuation zone, and a compact, low-water, low-neutron plant is well suited to siting near the loads it serves.
- [What is firm power?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-firm-power.html): Firm power is electricity that's available on demand, regardless of weather or time of day — the reliable backbone a grid needs behind intermittent renewables.
- [How does MetroVolt connect to the grid?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-metrovolt-connect-to-the-grid.html): MetroVolt's direct-energy converter produces a DC-native output, which is conditioned to feed the AC grid — or to serve large DC loads like data centers directly.
- [Can fusion make hydrogen or industrial heat?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-fusion-make-hydrogen-or-industrial-heat.html): Yes. Firm, DC-native fusion electricity is well suited to hydrogen electrolysis, and a fusion plant can supply high-grade industrial heat as well as power.
- [How do MetroVolt and AEGIS differ?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-do-metrovolt-and-aegis-differ.html): They share one architecture but serve different markets: MetroVolt is the commercial grid generator; AEGIS is the defense-oriented, resilient, islandable variant.
- [Is helium-3 really mined on the moon?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-helium-3-really-mined-on-the-moon.html): Lunar regolith genuinely holds helium-3, and it's a real long-horizon resource — but Kronos breeds its own helium-3 and does not depend on lunar mining.
- [Is fusion energy like a hydrogen bomb?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-fusion-energy-like-a-hydrogen-bomb.html): No. Both involve fusion, but a power plant holds only seconds of fuel in a controlled plasma that can't explode — the opposite of a bomb's uncontrolled release.
- [Why deuterium instead of ordinary hydrogen?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-deuterium-instead-of-hydrogen.html): Ordinary hydrogen fuses far too slowly for a power plant — it takes the sun billions of years. Deuterium fuses readily at temperatures a reactor can reach.
- [How is AI used in a fusion plant?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-is-ai-used-in-a-fusion-plant.html): Kronos designs an AI-native control stack — a real-time digital twin, anomaly detection, and deterministic safety clamps — to hold the plasma within its envelope faster than a human could.
- [What is a burning plasma?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-a-burning-plasma.html): A burning plasma is one where the energy from fusion itself provides a significant share of the heating. Kronos runs a driven burn, self-heated but sustained by external input.
- [How precisely must the plasma be controlled?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-precisely-must-the-plasma-be-controlled.html): Extremely — position, shape, and stability must be held on millisecond and microsecond timescales, which is why Kronos designs an automated, twin-assisted control stack.
- [Has anyone achieved net-energy fusion?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/has-anyone-achieved-net-energy-fusion.html): At the reaction level, yes — the 2022 NIF experiment released more fusion energy than the laser delivered to the target. Plant-level net energy, counting all inputs, has not yet been demonstrated.
- [Is fusion better than fission?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-fusion-better-than-fission.html): Fusion has a cleaner safety, waste, and fuel profile; fission is proven and deployed today. They're different tools — fusion aims to be the firm clean power fission promised, without its drawbacks.
- [Why not just build more solar and batteries?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-not-just-use-more-solar-and-batteries.html): Solar and batteries are essential but can't affordably cover multi-day and seasonal gaps. Firm clean generation like fusion complements them — it doesn't replace them.
- [What if the magnets fail?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-if-the-magnets-fail.html): A superconducting magnet failure (a quench) is a known, engineered-for event: detection systems safely dump the stored energy, and the plasma simply stops.
- [How long does a fusion plant last?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-long-does-a-fusion-plant-last.html): Kronos designs MetroVolt on a 30 full-power-year basis, and its low-neutron first wall lasts that full life without replacement.
- [What does it cost to run a fusion plant?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-does-it-cost-to-run-a-fusion-plant.html): Operating costs are low and stable: the fuel is cheap and abundant, there's no fuel-price volatility, and a low-neutron design avoids frequent component changeouts.
- [What intellectual property does Kronos hold?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-intellectual-property-does-kronos-hold.html): Kronos's core patent is the granted US 12,009,112 — the S.M.A.R.T. architecture — anchoring a broader portfolio of applications and trademarks held by its IP holding company.
- [How does Kronos validate its simulations?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-kronos-validate-its-simulations.html): Through a three-tier evidence discipline — reduced-order engines, deposited input decks, and referee-defense analyses — cross-validated and released openly as 81 analyses.

- [What is fusion's environmental impact?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-fusions-environmental-impact.html): Fusion has an unusually light footprint: no CO₂, no long-lived waste, little water and land, no fuel mining — with a low-neutron design lighter still.
- [Why does fusion energy matter?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-does-fusion-energy-matter.html): Fusion could supply firm, clean, abundant power — the always-on, low-carbon energy a decarbonizing world needs and doesn't yet have at scale.
- [What are the main approaches to fusion?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-are-the-main-approaches-to-fusion.html): The two big families are magnetic confinement (tokamaks and stellarators) and inertial confinement (lasers). Kronos is a compact, high-field magnetic tokamak.
- [How long have we been researching fusion?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-long-have-we-researched-fusion.html): Since the 1950s — about seven decades of steady progress, recently accelerated by high-temperature superconductors, private investment, and the 2022 ignition milestone.
- [Can fusion power ships or spacecraft?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/can-fusion-power-ships-or-spacecraft.html): Fusion's energy density makes it attractive for marine and space propulsion in the long term, but Kronos focuses on firm grid and defense power on Earth.
- [How does Kronos's business work?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/how-does-kronos-business-work.html): Kronos develops fusion generators and plans to earn revenue through power offtake, plant sales, and strategic and defense partnerships — publishing its work to build trust with those partners.
- [Is fusion safe for the people who work there?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/is-fusion-safe-for-workers.html): Yes. The hazards — magnet energy, tritium, activated components — are engineering-controlled with standard radiation-protection practice, and there's no accident-scale risk.
- [Why compact fusion?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/why-compact-fusion.html): High-temperature superconductors let a small, high-field machine reach reactor conditions — and smaller means cheaper, faster to build, and manufacturable at fleet scale.
- [What is a fusion pilot plant?](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/what-is-a-fusion-pilot-plant.html): A pilot plant is a smaller machine that carries the gate-adjudication mission before a full commercial unit. The Kronos FPP is a ~0.25 GW-class device at R₀ = 2.7 m.

### Comparisons & deep-dives
- [D–³He vs D–T Fuel](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/d-he3-vs-d-t.html): Deuterium–helium-3 versus deuterium–tritium: fewer neutrons, direct energy conversion, and a harder ignition problem. A side-by-side for the Kronos choice.
- [Negative vs Positive Triangularity](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/negative-vs-positive-triangularity.html): Positive triangularity buys easy H-mode access with dangerous ELMs; negative triangularity trades that for an ELM-free edge. Why Kronos picked the reverse shape.
- [Spherical vs Conventional Tokamak](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/spherical-vs-conventional-tokamak.html): Low aspect ratio versus the classic thin donut: how the spherical tokamak trades a crowded center column for better stability and pressure efficiency.
- [The 24.6 Tesla Magnet System](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-24-6-tesla-magnet-system.html): A deep-dive on the Kronos magnet: a graded, demountable REBCO winding pack at 24.6 T peak, sitting just above the demonstrated single-coil field and defended by a strain clamp.
- [Firm Fusion Power for Data Centers](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/firm-power-for-data-centers.html): AI data centers need large, constant, low-carbon power. A DC-native, low-neutron fusion generator is designed to match that load profile without a steam cycle.
- [D–³He vs p–¹¹B Fuel](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/d-he3-vs-p-11b.html): Both are advanced low-neutron fuels. D–³He is the achievable commercial baseline; p–¹¹B is truly aneutronic but harder — the gated endpoint of the Kronos roadmap.
- [Tokamak vs Stellarator](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/tokamak-vs-stellarator.html): Tokamaks use a plasma current for higher performance but risk disruptions; stellarators are disruption-free but complex. Kronos chose the tokamak and engineers around the disruption risk.
- [Direct Conversion vs the Steam Cycle](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/direct-conversion-vs-steam-cycle.html): Direct energy conversion turns charged particles straight into electricity; a steam cycle boils water for a turbine. DEC skips the Carnot limit and shrinks the plant.
- [HTS vs LTS Magnets](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/hts-vs-lts-magnets.html): High-temperature superconductors reach far higher fields and warmer temperatures than low-temperature ones — the difference that makes compact high-field fusion possible.
- [Fusion vs Geothermal](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-geothermal.html): Both are firm, clean, always-on power — but geothermal is limited to favorable geology, while a compact fusion plant can be sited near the load it serves.
- [Fusion vs Small Modular Reactors](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-small-modular-reactors.html): Both aim at firm, modular clean power — but SMRs are fission (spent fuel, proliferation, mined fuel) while fusion has no chain reaction and no long-lived waste.
- [Compact vs Conventional Fusion](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/compact-vs-conventional-fusion.html): Compact high-field machines reach reactor conditions in a fraction of the size of conventional low-field designs — cheaper, faster, and manufacturable as a product.
- [Magnetic vs Inertial Confinement](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/magnetic-vs-inertial-confinement.html): Magnetic confinement holds a steady plasma in a field; inertial confinement compresses fuel pellets with lasers in pulses. Kronos is magnetic — suited to continuous power.
- [Deep Dive: The Staged D–³He Fuel Cycle](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-staged-fuel-cycle-deep-dive.html): A walk through the Kronos fuel roadmap — from D–T commissioning through D-rich and ³He-rich D–³He to the p–¹¹B endpoint — and how the plant breeds its own helium-3.
- [Deep Dive: The Multi-Modal DEC Train](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-multi-modal-dec-train-deep-dive.html): Kronos layers four conversion modes — direct charged-particle, MHD, thermionic, and photonic — to reach a blended, gate-adjudicated efficiency without betting on one method.
- [Deep Dive: The Confinement Case](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-confinement-case-deep-dive.html): The design needs H98 = 1.84, resting on negative triangularity, the hot-ion posture, and density headroom — anchored on DIII-D data, with the nonlinear gyrokinetic check outstanding.
- [Fusion for Industrial Process Heat](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-industrial-heat.html): Industry needs vast firm heat, much of it fossil-fired today. A fusion plant can supply firm high-grade heat and power together — a route to decarbonizing hard-to-electrify industry.
- [Fusion for Green Hydrogen](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-green-hydrogen.html): Firm, DC-native fusion power is an ideal match for hydrogen electrolysis — high utilization and a direct DC feed make for cheaper, cleaner hydrogen.
- [Fusion for Desalination](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-desalination.html): Water-scarce regions need firm power (and sometimes heat) to desalinate at scale. A firm, low-water, siteable fusion plant is well matched to coastal desalination.
- [Fusion vs Coal](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-coal.html): Both are firm, always-on power — but coal emits CO₂ and pollution and depends on mining, while fusion is clean and runs on seawater-derived fuel.
- [Fusion vs Wind](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-wind.html): Wind is cheap but intermittent and land-hungry; fusion is firm and compact. They're complements — cheap intermittent power plus firm clean backup.
- [Fusion vs Battery Storage](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-battery-storage.html): Batteries shift energy across hours; fusion generates firm energy continuously. They solve different problems — and storage can't affordably cover multi-day or seasonal gaps.
- [Fusion vs Hydropower](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-hydropower.html): Hydropower is firm, clean, and dispatchable — but geographically limited and largely built out. Fusion offers the same firm clean profile without the geography constraint.
- [D–³He vs D–D](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/d-he3-vs-d-d.html): D–D is the reaction that breeds Kronos's helium-3; D–³He is the low-neutron reaction that produces most of the power. The catalyzed cycle uses both together.
- [Private vs Public Fusion](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/private-vs-public-fusion.html): Public programs pursue fusion as large-scale science; private companies pursue it as a compact, manufacturable product. Kronos is private — and builds on decades of public research.
- [Deep Dive: The Magnet & Shield System](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-magnet-and-shield-system-deep-dive.html): Kronos's 24.6 T graded, demountable REBCO magnet, its strain margin, and the compact center-stack shield that a low-neutron design makes possible.
- [Deep Dive: The Hot-Ion Posture](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-hot-ion-posture-deep-dive.html): Running the ions hotter than the electrons raises fusion output and limits losses — a real upside Kronos treats as a bet, with the near-thermal case as the operating baseline.
- [Deep Dive: The Open Questions](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-open-questions-deep-dive.html): Kronos publishes a ranked register of what it cannot yet claim — confinement verification, the hot-ion posture, divertor detachment, TAE transport, and runaway mitigation.
- [Deep Dive: The Economics of MetroVolt](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-economics-of-metrovolt.html): The full LCOE ladder — FOAK $84–92 to fleet $48–56/MWh — the required PPA, the cost floor, and why Kronos declines the sub-$40 figures others quote.
- [Deep Dive: The AI-Native Plant](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-ai-native-plant-deep-dive.html): An eight-layer control architecture — from microsecond deterministic reflexes to a predictive digital twin and human oversight — that holds the plasma within its safety envelope.
- [Deep Dive: Disruptions & Safety](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/disruptions-and-safety-deep-dive.html): How Kronos reduces disruption likelihood, budgets a 10 GJ event, and relies on fusion's passive safety — with runaway mitigation stated as an open item.
- [Fusion for AI Compute](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-ai-compute.html): AI training clusters are becoming gigawatt-scale, constant, DC loads — precisely the firm, DC-native power a MetroVolt plant is designed to deliver.
- [Fusion for Remote Microgrids](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-remote-microgrids.html): Remote and islanded sites depend on fuel convoys and diesel. A firm, islandable fusion generator gives them clean, self-sufficient power without a supply line.
- [Fusion for Defense Installations](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-defense-installations.html): Bases and mission-critical sites need power that survives grid outages and fuel-supply disruption. AEGIS is designed as firm, islandable, resilient power for exactly that.
- [Fusion vs Natural Gas](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-natural-gas.html): Gas is firm and flexible but emits CO₂ and methane and rides volatile fuel markets. Fusion offers the same firmness and flexibility with zero operating emissions and stable fuel.
- [Fusion vs Solar](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-solar.html): Solar is the cheapest energy ever built — but intermittent and land-hungry. Fusion is firm and compact. The clean grid needs both, in different roles.
- [Firm vs Intermittent Power](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/firm-vs-intermittent-power.html): Firm power is available on demand; intermittent power depends on weather. Understanding the difference explains why a clean grid needs both — and where fusion fits.
- [Helium-3: Terrestrial vs Lunar](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/helium-3-terrestrial-vs-lunar.html): Earth's helium-3 is scarce; the moon's is abundant but far. Kronos sidesteps both by breeding its own helium-3 in the plasma.
- [Baseload vs Dispatchable Power](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/baseload-vs-dispatchable-power.html): Baseload runs steadily; dispatchable follows demand. A fusion plant can do both — firm baseload that also modulates to help balance the grid.
- [Deep Dive: The Gate Program](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-gate-program-deep-dive.html): Kronos advances through pre-registered gates — G1 confinement, G2 magnet, G3 fuel loop — with a pilot plant between the testbed and the commercial unit.
- [Deep Dive: The Plant Power Flow](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-plant-power-flow-deep-dive.html): How 2.41 GW of fusion power becomes 0.61–0.84 GWe net — through the charged/neutron split, direct conversion, and the plant's own recirculating loads.
- [Deep Dive: The Divertor Challenge](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-divertor-challenge-deep-dive.html): Exhausting a fusion plasma's heat is one of the hardest engineering problems. Kronos relies on detachment and Super-X flux expansion — a demanding, honestly-stated requirement.
- [Deep Dive: Current Drive & Bootstrap](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/current-drive-and-bootstrap-deep-dive.html): Steady-state operation needs the plasma to supply most of its own current. Kronos reaches a bootstrap fraction of 0.59–0.78, topped up by non-inductive drive.
- [Deep Dive: The Vertical Stability Problem](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-vertical-stability-problem-deep-dive.html): A strongly elongated plasma is vertically unstable and must be actively held. Kronos runs κ = 2.56 with a corrected control margin of 2.02.
- [Deep Dive: Neutronics & Activation](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-neutronics-and-activation-deep-dive.html): How a low-neutron design transforms the shielding, material lifetime, and waste picture — with a high-fidelity OpenMC calculation planned to fix the numbers.
- [Fusion for District Heating](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-district-heating.html): City heating networks need firm, low-carbon heat. A fusion plant can supply both power and heat to a district system — with siting flexibility to sit near the city.
- [Fusion for Synthetic Fuels](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-synthetic-fuels.html): Synthetic e-fuels need vast firm clean electricity to be viable. Firm, DC-native fusion power at high utilization is a strong match for producing them.
- [Fusion for Shipping](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-shipping.html): Shipping is hard to decarbonize because fuel energy density matters at sea. Fusion's extraordinary energy density makes it a compelling long-horizon marine power source.
- [Deuterium vs Tritium](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/deuterium-vs-tritium.html): Deuterium is stable, abundant, and from seawater; tritium is radioactive, scarce, and must be bred. Kronos runs mostly deuterium with only a minimal tritium role.
- [Fusion vs Antimatter](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-antimatter.html): Antimatter has the highest energy density imaginable — but can't be produced or stored practically. Fusion is the most energy-dense source we can actually build.
- [Tokamaks vs Alternative Concepts](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/tokamaks-vs-alternative-concepts.html): Beyond tokamaks and stellarators are FRCs, spheromaks, and magnetic mirrors — each a different bet. The tokamak is the most developed, and Kronos's choice.
- [Copper vs Superconducting Magnets](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/copper-vs-superconducting-magnets.html): Copper magnets waste enormous power as heat and can't sustain high fields continuously. Superconducting magnets carry current losslessly — essential for a power reactor.
- [Fusion Fuel vs Fission Fuel](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-fuel-vs-fission-fuel.html): Fusion runs on hydrogen isotopes from seawater with no enrichment or proliferation risk; fission runs on mined, enriched uranium with spent-fuel and safeguards burdens.
- [Deep Dive: Plasma-Facing Materials](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/plasma-facing-materials-deep-dive.html): The first wall must survive heat, particle bombardment, and radiation for the plant's life. Kronos uses laser-fused hafnium carbide — and low-neutron fuel eases the burden.
- [Deep Dive: MHD Stability](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/mhd-stability-deep-dive.html): A survey of the stability limits Kronos must respect — kink, ballooning, vertical, and resistive-wall modes — and how the design stays inside them at β_N = 4.33.
- [Deep Dive: The ³He Mass Balance](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/the-he3-mass-balance-deep-dive.html): How Kronos breeds and recycles helium-3 in a staged, catalyzed cycle — and the honest ~33% fleet self-sufficiency gap the analyses report.

- [Fusion for Mining](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-mining.html): Mines are often remote, diesel-dependent, and electrifying fast. Firm, islandable fusion power could replace diesel and run electric mining fleets cleanly.
- [Fusion for Direct Air Capture](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-direct-air-capture.html): Pulling CO₂ from the air is enormously energy-intensive and only makes sense with cheap, firm, clean power — a natural application for firm fusion.
- [Fusion for Developing Economies](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-developing-economies.html): Firm clean power without fuel imports could let fast-growing economies electrify and industrialize cleanly — energy independence from a compact, siteable source.
- [Fusion & the Electrification of Everything](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-and-electrification.html): Electrifying transport, heat, and industry will multiply electricity demand — and much of that new demand needs to be firm and clean. That's fusion's market.
- [Fusion for Water Treatment](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-for-water-treatment.html): Desalination and advanced water treatment are energy-intensive. Firm, low-water fusion power could secure fresh water for arid and growing regions.

- [Fusion vs Fission Safety](https://kronosfusionenergy.com/learn/fusion-vs-fission-safety.html): Fission needs active safety systems to manage a chain reaction and decay heat; fusion fails safe by physics — no runaway, no meltdown, no evacuation zone.
